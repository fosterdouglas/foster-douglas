Games of (My) Year 2021 Games of
(My) Year 2021

As always, my "game of the year" lists include any games I played this year, not only 2021's new releases. It's always more important for me to enjoy, celebrate, and highlight notable experiences generally than to arbitrarily rush to play the newest games.

For me, 2021 was the year of indie gaming. At the start of the year, I made myself a goal to play 2 indie games per week (or average 2-per-week by the end of the year at least). My definition of indie was loose, but in most cases the games are at least an hour or more in length, generally (but not always) have an "end" state of some sort, and are considered "indie" by the generally accepted definition (not published by a large company, a moderate budget and team size, etc). I achieved the goal just barely, and played 105 total indie games (wow!), although I will say that it didn't feel like a "difficult" goal to achieve necessarily because of how varied and short the experiences were each week. This gives rise to a "Notable Indies" portion of the list below, which I think will be recurring in future lists.

And as a result, I played a good amount less "AAA" games in 2021, which was a necessary trade off. Generally speaking, I prefer indie games anyway, so I don't feel remorse about this. Below are quick blurbs on the notable experiences I had this year. Many of them are incomplete thoughts, or too-late recollections of thoughts I had during the original playthroughs (many months ago for some), but I try to give a general gist of why, to me, it's worth putting on a list:

Notable Indies

Ikenfell

Happy Ray Games

I played this early in the year, but I remember being wholly charmed by it. It has a unique turn-based combat system that feels like a simplified variation on Paper Mario and Tactics games, and the characters and narrative were memorable and interesting. If I were a kid now, I could even see myself enjoying it as much as I enjoyed Chrono Trigger as a kid (although of course Chrono Trigger is more seminal in every way, that distinction might be subtle to younger audiences).

Chicory: A Colorful Tale

Greg Lobanov, Finji

This game wears its 2D Zelda inspirations proudly on its sleeve, and in certain aspects it actually succeeds in ways that the 2D Zelda games don't entirely. Which is to say with its storytelling and emotional heart. It has much less "combat" (but by no means completely absent of conflict and tension), and I love the focus on character interaction and how unique the painting mechanic is. It's not just another hero carrying a sword and shield, but a vulnerable and grounded protagonist who grows with the player. Weirdly similar to my comment on Ikenfell, I could absolutely see this being an inspirational game for someone younger, and that in the future they would look back fondly on playing it as a child. If the Games of the Year section wasn't so packed already, this game would have probably made it there.

TOEM

Something We Made

A game all about taking photos. It's so charming, so easy to play and "cozy" feeling, has a great mix of limited exploration and satisfying collectibles, and the perfect overall length. Pulling off black and white seems so tricky normally but it pulls it off with distinction and is the perfect choice for the game, both visually and conceptually for a game based around photography to focus on composition and reference the roots of the medium.

Alba: A Wildlife Adventure

Ustwo, Plug In Digital

Another... game all about taking photos. This game hit me in some interesting ways, and I was surprised at how it had a purpose and "mission" beyond the game itself (broadly, it's about wildlife preservation), and how it gently preaches that mission to the player. It's also really interesting to play directly as a child, and I don't think most games that try this really pull it off but here its done with a lot of whimsy and care.

Everything

David OReilly

In some ways, this sometimes barely feels like a "game," and in other ways I consider it as a contender for best of the year. It's a great example of effective organic exploration design. It's also in the rare category of explicitly incorporating philosophy in its narrative, and the even rarer category of that philosophy being delivered directly by Alan Watts. As with all the games in the Games of the Year group, even though this one isn't, I could absolutely see replaying this some day in the future, if for no other reason than an excuse to listen to a couple of hours of Alan Watts philosophize over top a Katamari-infused universe simulator.

Void Bastards

Blue Manchu

It's hard to pinpoint exactly why I enjoyed this game so much. I do know that all the systems feel exactly as complex as they need to be, and that the gameplay loop is challenging but fair and varied enough to stay interesting for the 10 hours or so it takes to finish a single run. The humor and art style are also spot on.

Notable Strategy

Desperadoes III

Mimimi Games

The strategy game design was both satisfyingly open-ended, allowing dozens of solutions to any given area, while also still retaining direction and momentum forward. I found myself often surprised at how creative I could be, and sometimes needed to be, to get through certain sections and accomplish some of the optional goals too. Also surprisingly, the narrative was quite interesting, though not ground-breaking, and felt like a step forward from their previous title, Shadow Tactics.

Into the Breach

Subset Games

From the people that did FTL, this is a thoughtful experience with meticulously designed turn-based strategy and endless variation. I only played through the game once, despite the game heavily encouraging multiple playthroughs. That's actually my only complaint with the game, that it could have maybe strung together a few sets of the 4 islands to create something more like a standard campaign to play through, instead of individual disconnected runs. Regardless, one of the best modern strategy games I've maybe ever played.

Frostpunk

11 Bit Studios

A complex and tense game with a pretty narrow design for viable strategies, but still a lot fun regardless. In particular, the "prequel" DLC that was released was my favorite scenario of the lot because it deviated the most from the rest of the game's stricter style. I'm glad the game was successful enough to warrant a sequel eventually, which was recently announced. I'm also very excited for the board game adaptation that's coming.

Slay the Spire

Mega Crit Games

In the category of "rougelike" games with loads of relics to collect to then use to break the rules, this game lives next to the greatest games of this style like Dead Cells , The Binding of Isaac , and Risk of Rain, except... it's a card-based strategy game. It's since paved the way for an entire genre of card-action games (which are distinct from what Hearthstone does, I think). I played it much less than I was tempted to, but I did play through to the end boss with the three primary classes and thoroughly enjoyed its deep mechanics.

Most Addictive

Planet Coaster

Fontier Developments

This was my Pandemic comfort game, for sure. I put 100 hours into it (generally rare for me for a single game), and really enjoyed creating coasters and micromanaging the aspects of the parks and employees. It feels like an optimized balance loop between strategy and creativity, creating something unique in your park and seeing how that affects the ecosystem and then adapting and doing that process over and over again. It feels like a worthy successor to the Rollercoaster Tycoon series, and is actually a better experience in most ways.

Two Point Hospital

Two Point Studios

Similarly, this game is clearly inspired by Bullfrog's classic Theme Hospital from the 90s, a goofy-in-tone but serious-strategy hospital management sim. This game takes that goofy tone even further, which works really well. My only complaint with the experience is that once you realize how important the room decorations that level up each room in the hospital are, it feels like you can get through things a lot easier in ways that maybe didn't feel intended in the design. I'm definitely looking forward to the team's university-themed follow-up this year, Two Point Campus.

Notable Co-Op

Ashen

A44

During the first boss of this, I was worried that we weren't cut out for this type of game, but after some practice it ended up being very satisfying. It implements co-op in a nice way, slightly more intuitively than FromSoftware games generally do. The art style and world design is really satisfying and simple, and exploring some of the nooks and crannies of the moderately-sized open world felt rewarding.

Sackboy: A Big Adventure

Sumo Digital

This was a complete joy to play local co-op, and felt like a really nice transition for Sackboy from side-scrolling to 3D adventure. It feels as though a lot of the structural groundwork was laid with Tearaway, although I know a separate developer did most of the work on this entry.

Fallout 76

Bethesda Softworks

I consider myself a mild Fallout fan, although I've recently realized that I've played Fallout 3 , New Vegas , Shelter , Fallout 4 , and now Fallout 76, so maybe I like the series more than I think I do. I might have had a little more fun with this than Lance did, especially because I did most of the "base building" stuff, which I found a lot of enjoyment with. The game's narrative builds toward a nice climax and some of the characters are memorable. It's worth noting that we played this long after it was released, after it had a lot of patches and full-blown content updates that were absent at launch.

Embr

Muse Games

Adding this here for the sheer novelty factor of the experience. It was a lot of fun to play as a firefighter with some cartoon whimsy, and have some unique "gadgets" that each firefighter could bring into the field. The boss design was pretty poorly done, but luckily there were only a few bosses anyway.

Operation: Tango

Clever Plays

A surprisingly fun asynchronous game where one person plays as an "agent" in the field, while the other person is the tech "hacker" off site. It had really fun puzzles, great overall length, and some very nicely balanced and lenient failure conditions. It created some of the most stressful and hilarious co-op moments of the year, and we actually played through it twice to see both sides of each level.

Heroes of Hammerwatch

CrackShell

I was very surprised by how addicting the gameplay loop was for this. A given "run" had an interesting difficulty curve that goes from Easy > Difficult > Very Difficult > Easy, if you can find enough interesting artifacts along the way. It feels very well suited for co-op as well, playing it single player seems a lot less dynamic and interesting.

Nioh 2

Team Ninja

Pretty much an improvement in every way over the first Nioh. One of the few games available at 120fps on PS5 near launch, it has some beautiful environments and is incredibly silky smooth. It's always interesting to dive into these games with complex systems that take some time to fully understand, and generally it pays off later on. We enjoyed it enough to play the three DLCs as well.

Most Surprising

Tearaway Unfolded

Media Molecule

This has all the charm of a Media Molecule Littlebigplanet entry, but as a third-person adventure story. I'm not sure why I was so surprised by this, but the game had an incredible amount of creativity in how it used the PS4's controller, and how it incorporates the "You" (the player) into the game in interesting meta ways. Although the game was about twice as long as it needed to be for my taste, all of it still feels fresh and innovative throughout.

Dragon Quest XI S: Echoes of an Elusive Age

Square Enix

Having never played a DQ game and sort of insinuating what they'd be like based on some random impressions, this game was a massive surprise to me (although it may be the case that it is the least Dragon Quest-like entry in the series). The production value, and in particular the music, is astonishing. It tries a lot of unique things and succeeds with most of them, and it really feels like a nice evolution or hybridization of a classic JRPG.

Most Disappointing

The Pathless

Giant Squid

I know that this game is beloved by a lot of fans, and I can definitely see why, but to me the game design feels ham-fisted and disjointed. The open world is beautiful and moving through it fluidly and "flow"-like feels really special. The decision to then make it into an Ubisoft-style "activate the towers" game design is a bit disappointing, but not what soiled the experience for me. It's the boss design that feels unnecessary (although I've seen plenty of discourse heavily praising it). It doesn't feel like it fits with the vibe of the world, almost as if an executive came in at the end of development and said "this is great, but you know what it's missing? BOSSES." So much else about the game is experimental that I feel they could have leaned even harder into it to better tie it all together for me.

Sayonara Wild Hearts

Simogo

Outside of all of the unfortunate controversy around the development team on this game, which sullies its appeal as it is, I came away after playing it with a weird mix of inspired and frustrated. My primary issue with the gameplay is that it doesn't encourage experimentation. The game has a notable and stellar soundtrack, and the game feels at its best when the player can get into a flow with the music and traverse the levels. But, with such strict level design in some cases, you'll find yourself replaying sections of levels over and over to get past them. While there's nothing wrong with that in theory, being restarted at checkpoints feels deeply at odds with the flow of the gameplay style.

Maquette

Graceful Decay

It's easy to sum this up, which is to say a very cool idea executed a bit poorly. It's more focused on telling a (heartfelt, for sure) sob-story about a breakup someone had than it is concerned with the puzzle-designed experience of the player, unfortunately. If this type of game is interesting to you, check out Superliminal, Manifold Garden, or The Pedestrian, just a few of many great games in the puzzle genre that I played in 2021.

Raji: An Ancient Epic

Nodding Head Games

Unfortunately, this experience didn't quite come together for me. The controls and combat design are incredibly frustrating, to the extreme that it felt borderline unplayable at times personally. The visuals are great, and the story-telling style and setting are quite unique and distinct, but neither is enough to carry the entire experience.

Outer Wilds: Echoes of the Eye

Mobius Digital, Annapurna Interactive

This doesn't technically belong in this section, but I had such a polarizing experience with Outer Wilds' DLC that it's worth pointing out. The first hour or two of this 8+-hour DLC was mind-blowing and somehow manages to up the ante on what was one of the best games I've ever played as is. Unfortunately (for me), it soon devolves into a (admittedly well-designed) horror-survival experience, in ways that the original game never even came close to being. It was soon obvious to me that there was no way I was going to enjoy getting through the experience, so I watched a playthrough of it instead. There is so much positive to say about this expansion, and there are three distinct "oh my gosh" revelations which each one-up the other in ways that are so incredible and inspiring and unforgettable. There is absolutely a reality where this game could have had a mode without the aspect that made it survival-horror, but I do believe I'm the minority in this opinion as most online discourse seems to enjoy the specific mechanic I felt frustrated by.

Games of My Year

Generally speaking, my criteria for GOTY is pretty loose, although one common theme that I personally have noticed is: if by the end of a given experience I am thinking "I can't wait to play this again someday" then there's a good chance it belongs in this list. This is particularly true because I personally very rarely replay games. I also try not to limit this list to any specific length (because, well, some years you just play a lot of great games), while also trying to resist the temptation to just put 15+ games here.

Disco Elysium

Robert Kurvitz

There is way more to say about this game than I could ever conjure into words. It really feels like it speaks for itself entirely. Every moment is packed with emotion and dripping with style, and it is so consistent with itself that it feels almost like it was birthed whole-cloth as a single entity.

The city the game takes place in, and the exposed lore of the world that this city lives amongst, and all of its people and their place in that world all feel so real. Every "quest" you come across never feels as such, but just another person in this world living their life and integrating you into it in the ways that they feel like doing. It's also worth noting that I generally dislike the "amnesia" main protagonist trope, but this game builds its entire premise and plot on it which makes it feel believable and worthy. It also can't be overstated how impactful the soundtrack is in Disco Elysium. It is another character in the story and the world, it ties everything together, and it makes you feel everything Harry feels.

In my opinion, this might be one of the best games ever crafted, and it will no doubt go down in the annals of history as a seminal classic of interactive fiction and creativity. I can't wait to play it again some day and explore the hundreds of decisions and options I didn't stumble across the first time around.

Returnal

Housemarque

I don't think I've ever been as deeply immersed in a game as I was with Returnal. From a flow-state gameplay perspective absolutely, but also as a can't-stop-thinking-about-it brain worm life feeling (coincidentally though, Disco Elysium may be the only other game I've ever played that gave me that same feeling). I finished the experience across 6 intensely condensed days of playing, and there was once that I think I launched and played the game 4 separate times in a single day (admittedly for shorter sessions each, but its worth noting how unlike me it is to do this). The world immersion and the extreme precision and care put into the game design flow itself mostly contributed to my not being able to stop thinking about it.

After such wonderful gameplay systems and world design, everything else about the game feels like icing on the cake. The particle effect design, the ear-candy sound design, the creature models, the random-but-not-too-random level generation, and the punishing-but-forgiving-enough play loop to name a few. Then it's all capped off beautifully by its what-the-hell-is-going-on narrative and our main protagonist's great voice work. I didn't get the true "Act 3" ending, but I'll likely revisit the game in a few years and see that conclusion through.

Mutazione

Die Gute Fabrik

This game embodies so many of the reasons I play games. Games that allow us to escape to different worlds, with their own problems but also their own beauties that our own world can't have. Games that let us become somebody we aren't (in this case, a teenage girl going on a coming-of-age summer vacation trip to an irradiated fallout island full of quirky mutated humans...). Games that give us feelings and connections we can't otherwise find in our day-to-day lives. Games that create a space for us to laugh or cry or think or just be and feel. Games that manifest meaning from nothing, carefully weaving together an intricate pattern of sound and visual and text with interaction. Mutazione accomplishes all of this and so much more, and all with some beautifully simple gameplay mechanics.

"DEATHLOOP"

Arkane Studios

I expected this game to be much more like "Dishonored but with a time-loop" than it ends up being in reality. To be fair, it shares so many elements with Dishonored , so I can see why I might have thought that. But, the entire structure, tone, and even much of the moment-to-moment gameplay, is quite different. The DNA though is clearly there, and the level design is unquestionably descendent. And let's be honest, the level design is the shining star of the Dishonored franchise anyway.

It's well established online why everybody thinks so highly of this game, and it's all spot on. The world design is distinctive, and the cast of characters are quite memorable despite the player interacting directly with some of them very infrequently. It all feels so well realized and consistent with its bizarre and captivating sci-fi premise. My experience overall felt complete, so I may never actually revisit this game's campaign, but I loved my time with it and I could see them doing some DLC for it which would be a welcome addition.

Manifold Garden

William Chyr Studio

The mind-bending visuals of this game are enough to set it apart from many other games, but the puzzle design and bizarre avant-garde approach to story telling elevate it further. It's the rare case where I actually feel as though I'm playing as myself in this game, as it makes no effort to give the game's avatar any story, context, or personality. It's a direct connection between me and the game, facilitated through an interactive first-person camera control.

Experiencing Manifold Garden gave me the same feeling that you might get from an indie film, one that you feel is so quirky or specific or meaningful that it could have only been created specifically for you. The game expresses itself in such an experimental way, and entirely succeeds at making you think about things you never expected to think about from a game with no text or explicit narrative.

Honorable Mentions

Control

Remedy Entertainment

Jesse is such a well-written character and the world of Control is such an interesting blend of so many sci-fi tropes and still emerges as something completely distinct. Even a year after playing it, I still remember the names of the characters and a lot of the specifics of what happens in the story. It also feels like a really meaningful evolution of the "documents" and "audio logs" trope that a lot of these games fall into; it never feels like those items scattered about are "lore dump" but are a very meaningful way of world building. I can't wait to see what Remedy does next, although I don't imagine I'll be playing the suspense-turned-horror Alan Wake 2, unfortunately.

Diablo II: Resurrected

Blizzard Entertainment, Vicarious Visions

I played this once through with my brother, and can say that the team that Blizzard gave this to for the remastering treatment did great with it. I could see going back to this again with others or to play through on the harder difficulties. It's fittingly so that the best thing Blizzard has put out in quite a while is a remake of their pièce de résistance from 20 years ago.

Marvel's Spider-Man: Miles Morales

Insomniac Games

This sequel improved upon everything from the original in my opinion, and will go down certainly as the best "launch" title for the PS5 generation. It felt significantly more focused than the first (as in, many less collectibles), which I prefer in general in these open world games. More like Horizon: Zero Dawn (or Breath of the Wild maybe) and less like Assassin's Creed is always what I prefer. The story was just as good, and although the game length was much shorter, I almost always prefer compact and focused narrative experiences that players can and do actually finish.

13 Sentinels: Aegis Rim

Vanillaware

A truly bizarre and memorable sci-fi experience. The flow between the narrative and strategy sections never quite hit the mark for me, but overall the writing and the world they create manages to be both referential to many Japanese tropes while also feeling fresh and self-aware.
